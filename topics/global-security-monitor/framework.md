# Global Security Monitor

**Purpose:** Provide a neutral OSINT-style briefing on public global-security developments without using private watchlists, private notes, or personally revealing source rankings.

---

## Trusted Sources

Use source categories rather than a personal hierarchy of named accounts.

1. **Official public sources**
   - Government, military, diplomatic, emergency-management, and intergovernmental public statements.
   - Useful for confirmed positions, public warnings, sanctions, travel advisories, and operational claims.
   - Caveat: official sources can be incomplete, delayed, selective, or strategic.

2. **Established reporting organizations**
   - Wire services, major international outlets, reputable local outlets, and specialist publications with named reporters and editorial standards.
   - Useful for corroborated developments, timelines, and context.
   - Caveat: early reports can conflict; prefer updates that cite named officials, documents, or on-the-ground reporting.

3. **Specialist public-source analysts and trackers**
   - Domain experts who document methods, cite sources, and distinguish observation from inference.
   - Useful for geolocation, logistics indicators, infrastructure impacts, and conflict-adjacent developments.
   - Caveat: do not treat a single analyst post as confirmation without corroboration.

4. **General public search**
   - Search targets: official statements, public advisories, humanitarian updates, infrastructure notices, market or shipping disruptions, local reporting, and reputable situation updates.
   - Exclusions: private accounts, leaked material, graphic content, unverifiable claims, and engagement bait.

---

## Source Confidence Rules

- Prefer official/public reporting and corroborated specialist reporting.
- Separate confirmed developments from claims that remain unconfirmed, disputed, or single-source.
- State confidence plainly: confirmed, reported, claimed, unverified, disputed, or unclear.
- Do not sensationalize or use dramatic language.
- Do not infer intent, casualty counts, territorial control, or escalation from a single source.
- Avoid graphic detail unless essential to understanding a material development.
- Treat viral repetition as noise unless it adds a new source or verifiable evidence.

---

## Core Framework

### The fundamental question

What materially changed in the global-security picture since the last briefing, and does it alter risk, escalation, humanitarian impact, diplomatic posture, infrastructure resilience, market exposure, or near-term watch priorities?

### Scorecard or map

- **Confirmed developments:** publicly corroborated events, official statements, or reliable reporting.
- **Developing claims:** relevant but not yet corroborated; include only with clear uncertainty language.
- **Operational impact:** infrastructure, logistics, shipping, energy, airspace, cyber, humanitarian, or regional-spillover implications.
- **Diplomatic and policy response:** sanctions, talks, public warnings, aid announcements, statements, or institutional actions.
- **Signals to watch:** developments that would change risk assessment if corroborated.

### Key drivers

- Material events with reliable public sourcing.
- Shifts in official posture or policy.
- Infrastructure, logistics, energy, cyber, humanitarian, or market disruptions.
- Corroborated specialist analysis that changes the interpretation of known facts.

## Open Threads / Things to Watch

- Claims that need corroboration from official, local, or specialist public sources.
- Conflicting accounts that require careful confidence language.
- Infrastructure, energy, shipping, airspace, cyber, or humanitarian impacts.
- Diplomatic or policy moves that indicate escalation, de-escalation, or a change in posture.
- Developments that are widely shared but still unverified.

---

## Format Notes For Daily Briefings

- Lead with the most material confirmed change.
- Keep unconfirmed claims below confirmed developments and label them clearly.
- Include source links for material claims.
- Do not sensationalize, dramatize, or overstate certainty.
- Avoid graphic details unless necessary for understanding the public development.
- Emphasize what changed, why it matters, and what to watch next.
- If there is no material update, say so briefly and avoid filling space with recycled claims.
